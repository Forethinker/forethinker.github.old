My Simplified Investment Guide for Retirement

March 29, 2014 [ life ] #investment, #finance, #retirement Read in 8 min.

I have at least a dozen books on investment and for me, they boil down to the summary below:

Summary Chart

 |                     | Roth 401K | Roth IRA           | HSA           | 401K              |
 | Contributions       | Taxed     | Taxed              | Not Taxed     | Not Taxed         |
 | Lowers AGI          | No        | No                 | Yes           | Yes               |
 | Withdrawals         | Not Taxed | Not Taxed          | Not for med.  | Taxed             |
 | Limit (/>50)        | 17.5/23k  | 5.5/6.5k           | 33/65k        | 52k/57.5k (Taxed) |
 | Income Limits (S/M) | None      | 112-127/178-188k   | None          | None              |
 | Distribution        | 59.5      | 59.5               | 65            | 59.5              |
 | Required Minimum    | 70.5      | None               | None          | 70.5              |
 | Maintained by       | Employer  | Self               | Self/Employer | Employer          |
 | Early Withdrawals   | 10%       | 10% non-prin < 5yr | 20%           | 10%               |
 | Special Note        |           | 60 days loaning    |               | CA Muni Bonds     |

The Summary

  • Pay off the debt first, exception being your student loan or family loan which can have a lower rate of interest than a typical investment plan (~7% on average, ~5% conservative).
  • Any employer contribution is free money, but don't forego 401k contribution even if there isn't any.
  • If you are young and expect your income level will be higher in the future, max out Roth contribution first.
  • Take advantage of IRA if you think you will be a top-income earner one day, because you won't be even have this option anymore when you are the top income level (~97 percentile for single, 90 for joint, see the numbers in the above chart).
  • Choose Non-Roth option if you are old and/or are pessimistic about your career, or if you think the tax will be lower in the future.
  • If you have extra money, try to invest in your self-growth: especially in your education, career, or business.
  • If you still have money left for no immediate use, then go for a brokerage account. I recommend any index funds instead of tax-saving vehicles at this point, because you need some liquidity.
  • Use Health Savings Account as an extra tax efficient vehicle. It is less flexible than IRA, the penalty is higher if you withdraw early. I would only recommend it if you know you have imminent or foresesable medical expenses. A good bookeeping skill required for all the medical expenses.
  • For any conversion or withdrawal, do it when you are in a lower tax bracket.
  • Since I take a more risk in my retirement accounts, I take a safe approach for my short-term savings. Certificate of Deposit (CD) or Bonds are recommended by most people for short-term savings, but these people are not looking hard enough. With inflation, you could actually end up losing money. Look for High-Yield Savings Accounts (the rate might be the same, but it is more liquid) online, or High-yield Checking Accounts that might be available at a local Credit Union.
  • Credit cards offers and deals so far has given me better returns (for my time) than their points system. I keep them around for my credit score.
  • Contribution deadline is April 15.

If you feel like you are saving too much into your retirement accounts, you should think otherwise. There are exceptions to 10% IRS penalty called Substantially Equal Periodic Payment (SEPP). You can tap into your retirement accounts as early as when you are 35 years old. The are three methods of calculation for the distribution amount. Don't do SEPP on Roth though, as it gets taxed as income.

Another way to avoid it penalty is when you buy a house for the first time. If you (and your spouse) have not owned a home for two years, you can contribute up to 10,000 per person (up to $20,000 with your spouse's contribution) to your "home" (principal place of residence).

I think trying to beat the market is not worth your time, but should you decide to put your money into the hands of fund managers, go for small cap equity. Here is an excellent article titled "Index versus active? Nobel Prize professors can't agree" that gave me the insight. Past performance is not an indicator of future performance, but I think it makes sense that fund managers could leverage their connections and knowledge to make significant gains in small cap companies.

Invest early and focus on savings rate rather than making smart moves. Keep things simple and forget about it for 360 days in a year. But if you like learning about this stuff, go nuts like me.

When you read through investment forum threads skim through the contents that does not have any number or chart, otherwise you might be filling up your brain with unnecessary sob stories.

IRA == Emergency Funds

This is pretty much for those who have maxed out their 401k contribution.

  • If you want to use Roth IRA as your emergency fund, contribute to it directly. This allows you to withdraw the principal at any time.
  • Then do direct contribution to traditional 401k and then do the rollover, but note that this money will not be liquid for 5 years.
  • If you cumulate it, you can use it for the first-time home purchase for you or your wife, your children, or your ancestors, but only once.
  • Roth IRA withdrawal on the contribution is exempt from tax on earning if the account has been open for more than 5 years.
  • You can use IRA as an emergency fund but if you want to keep the tax savings, you have to put the money back in 60 days or 120 days for exceptions outlined below. This can be done once every 12 months., but this is risky and not recommended. (Stock Tanks -> Recession -> Lose Job -> Tab Emergency Fund -> Sell stocks at the worst time)
  • Rollover taxed 401k to Roth IRA as the last resort for tax shelter. It is the least efficient but has the highest limit.

Withdrawals that does not incur penalty before the age of 59.5:

  1. IRA owner's disability.
  2. IRA owner's death.
  3. Are a series of "substantially equal periodic payments" made over the life expectancy of the IRA owner.
  4. unreimbursed medical expenses that exceed 7 1/2% of adjusted gross income (AGI).
  5. medical insurance premiums after the IRA owner has received unemployment compensation for more than 12 weeks.
  6. the costs of a first-time home purchase (subject to a lifetime limit of $10,000).
  7. for the qualified expenses of higher education for the IRA owner and/or eligible family members.
  8. back taxes because of an Internal Revenue Service levy placed against the IRA.

First Time Home Buyer

So with all the rules above, the withdrawal will be in the order of 1. Maximum amount of principal I have put in directly. (e.g. ~$27500 for 5 years) 2. Up to $10,000 on the earnings 3. Any contribution, (not earnings) that is over 5 years old 4. Start paying 10% income tax on the rest

A little extra - Health Savings Account

This is really a healthy person's insurance, and I am debating whether I should commit to maxing out, because of the bookkeeping I have to do on my medical expenses and the inflexibility in transfer of funds. I just trying to convince myself that this will force me to have a good financial habits and skils, which might become necessary as my investment grows. But I worry that it might be eating too much of my time. Here is the best infographic/summary I found on the web. Read this first. Here is a small comparison chart for HSA providers. Some big banks like Chase and Bank of America also offer HSA, but you'd be surprised to find a bigger fees (than alternatives) when you search deeper. So my recommendation is HSA bank because of their low monthly fee for low-balance accounts and then it gets waived after ~$5000 in the account. Some attractive investment options are provided through TD Ameritrade. This is the best tax shelter after you have maxed out on your investment account. It might be THE best if you know your medical expenses will be high, because your contribution to HSA via payroll deduction is pre-tax and gives you savings on your FICA taxes - Social Security and Medical, if your employer does it for you. There is a limit to the contributions, however. Regarding the tax issue (e.g. the company offers HRA, but not HSA, so you don't get FICA savings, etc), your best guide is this website.

  • You cannot double dip on HRA and HSA. Couldn't find a source online, but I confirmed it with a financial advisor at my credit union. Don't try because IRS know.
  • Some more strategy for Californians who get taxed on HRA at the state level: Invest in Treasury Bonds or TIPS to avoid paying California Tax. Your overall return will be similar to your other investment account due since this is going to be no tax, low return investment.
  • Do not worry about making voluntary contribution, because they are tax-deductable.. So there is no requirement for it to be payroll deduction.

Picking Portfolio

  • For your portfolio, choose a mixture as you see fit, but watch out for tax and fees You could throw darts on a list of funds, but reading some news about the interest rates, the fiscal policy, the monetary policy and the world economy should help.
  • Be more conservative and have more bonds as you get older.

Personal Strategy (subject to change)

  • Roth 401k, Roth IRA, HSA, 401k rolled over to Roth IRA
  • Any excessive money / windfall / real estate opportunties -> mortgage
