Backup strategy

November 23, 2015 [ hack ] Read in 3 min.

The following assumes that you have encrypted data, which you should!

NAS

Synology owns in this market. There are free alternatives like XPenology or FreeNAS, but many warned about the lack of security, reliability, and simplicity in use. Pros:

  • features: there are tones of features you can take advantage of: RAID, encryption, and third party backup solution compatibility
  • simplicity: you set it once and let it do its job

Cons:

  • cost: a good brand NAS like Synology will cost you at least $200 for an entry-level 2-bay NAS without disks

RAID enabled enclosure

Not really a backup strategy, but the idea of running RAID1 on my external hard drive seemed pretty cool... until I read people's experiences.

Pros:

  • cost: usually the hard drives come bundled up and they are not such a bad price. The enclosure alone will cost about $75.
  • simplicity: if you want just encryption and RAID1, there is not really any setup you need to do.

Cons: - recovery: the option is limited for hardware-encrypted drives in case there is a failure.

Online backup

I think Crashplan is the most popular option with its attractive pricing option and reliability.

Pros:

  • reliability: most backup services uses Amazon's infrastructure or their own propriety infrastructure that provides a reliable redundancy.

Cons:

  • cost: you will pay just about buying new drives every four years or so. Which is not terribly bad but the cost can be very costly if you try to recover data via physical copy of the disk.
  • Time: Uploading/Downloading everything will take forever.
  • privacy: You have your data in the cloud and despite the encryption there is always some privacy concern. If you encrypt your data before uploading, you lose the versioning features of the service.

Hard drive duplicator

This is popular for some reason, but I found it pretty much unusable for my case.

Pros:

  • easy: just plug in the drive and click "start"
  • predictability: sector-by-sector copy will take the same time every time

Cons:

  • disk size: the source drive has to be smaller than the destination drive. This may end up costing you more money if you want to upgrade the size of the disk.
  • connectivity: most external drives these days have USB connector bolted on instead of SATA connectors. And there is no USB to SATA connector available in the market. You will have to stick with internal hard drives.
  • not interruptible: The operation cannot be interrupted in between.
  • interoperability: the hard drives will end up with the same signature so they both cannot be connected at the same time.
  • time-consuming: the copy happens for the entire drive regardless of the data you used, so the operation could take a half-day, which, for me was unacceptable.

Manual

Use a computer to mount decrypted drives before syncing them via robocopy or rsync

Pros:

  • flexible: you can do whatever you want

Cons:

  • complicated: you need to decrypt first
  • CPU intensive: decrypting the drive and copying requires a dedicated computer
  • Sync schedule gaps: between the times you actually sync the drives, there is a chance that there is a drive failure and you lose some data. This happened to me.
