Network Attached Storage experiments

November 23, 2015 [ hack ] Read in 4 min.

I just bought Synology DS215j. It was the best for the buck if I just ditch the video decoding

Reason for a local backup

Simple. I make a backup of my partition so that in case of theft, loss of my device, or a hard drive failure, I could get back my working system setup immediately. The size of these backups are pretty large and I don't want to risk these backups not being uploaded completely to the off-site. Since my primary machine is a laptop and the availability of high-speed internet is not always guaranteed, I want to a backup solution that is local and fast.

Router with USB support

Modern day routers have CPUs that are comparable to a dedicated NAS server and the best ones could maximize the external hard drives' throughput. But don't fall into the temptation. At best you will have a cheap NAS solution that will be seriously limited compared to a dedicated solution.

Here are a few things that a dedicated NAS wins on top of my head:

  • encryption: There isn't any for DD-WRT or Tomato
  • RAID: Once again, there isn't any for DD-WRT. You could possible pair the router with RAID external closure, but then it will have its own limitation. If there is a drive failure, you cannot access the remaining working drive with another enclosure, because the encryption is hardware-based and not software-based
  • synchronization with existing cloud solutions: you can install Crashplan's headless client or Dropbox module to make your workflow better, or Amazon Glazier backup for a cheap off-site backup.
  • media support: a true media solution does not really exist in the free solution
  • accessibility: you will have nice GUI on your web browser and your phone
  • easy setup and update: enough said

Mac Mini

Didn't want to leave the Apple folks out of the discussion and this post compares the pros/cons very well.

2-bay vs 4-bay

cantbelieveitsbacon at reddit summarizes it well for me.

  • 2 bay systems are simpler and easier to keep consistent across the board. When things fail (and they will) you will be happy that things that need fixing are simple and consistent.
  • RAID 1 reliabilty > RAID 5 reliability
  • RAID 1 recovery is trivial. RAID 5 recovery is tricky due to striping.
  • Drive replication for offsite storage is trivial on a 2-bay NAS: plug a new drive until it syncs, remove it, ship offsite. Can't do that with striped drives.
  • NAS hardware generations improve faster than we can fill up drives.
  • HDD cost falls faster than the HDD failure rate. It's more cost-effective to buy pairs of ever-better drives on an "as needed" basis.
  • RAID 1 RW throughput is sufficient for our purpose.

XPenology

This was a tempting for me. Hacking nature inside of me wanted to just tinker with a new box a bit. But I ultimately decided it is not worth my time and I think the cost benefit really kicks in when I build a large NAS with a lot of hard drives to take advantage of a higher order RAID configuration and need CPU intensive tasks like transcoding videos. But if your use case is simple and your needs are modest, going with Synology is better. Otherwise, your NAS will end up being overkill or too costly for the actual usage. For example, I could turn a laptop into a NAS by replacing the optical drive bay with additional 2.5 hard drive, but then my NAS will not saturate the quad core or the 8GB RAM it has. I would much rather turn it into a hackintosh than a NAS.

Which drives?

I just went with two WD Red Non-Pro drives from eBay for less than $70 each. For about the same price you can snag Hitach's 3TB refurbished drives that have better reliability rates and that will be my choice next time. I just didn't research enough before I bought the drives. I will probably still benefit from these low wattage and quiet drives. If I really want to be cost efficient though, I will get green drives and run wdiddle3 to turn off idle spin-down and use them. Western Digital does too much marketing. It is working, though, because it fooled me.

Hard drive speeds

This depends on your needs, but slower drives are cheaper, quieter, and more reliable than the faster drives. I think I lean towards slower speed drives because I tend to work with smaller file sizes on my local machine and I prefer quietness of the device.
