What should I strive to do as a young software engineer?

July 13, 2014 [ life ] #career, #engineer Read in 4 min.

In college, I took classes that interested me and I think it was the best choice I made for my education. People do not look at my grades anymore and a lot of knowledge I gained in college turned out to be exercise for my brain. So what I see is that the depth of your knowledge pays, but the breadth of your knowledge pays even more if you know how to knit them together.

In college, I lived as busy as I could be and I had not much room to juggle around once I set the schedule for the quarter. Since the graduation, however, things have changed. I am truly the boss of my life. Right after college, I spent all my time exploring the tools for my trade. I recently spent a good amount of time to form good habits for my health and finance. Now I am looking for something else. You can only stimulate brain so much by doing your job at work. You need to feed yourself with work that will give you the guidance on who you want to be and feel accomplished in things you care about.

I see the following career paths as a software engineer and these are my advices:

Engineer:

Engineers tend to get either enamored by million different ideas or get pulled by other people who demand their skills, services, and advices. But I think the best time spent for any software engineer is learning algorithms. You will probably safe-proof any job hunting you might need in the future. It can be fun and stimulating to tackle these problems!

Manager:

Good managerial skills are not restricted to people who pay 50k a year. You can start reading about it and start practicing right away. If you really think an MBA degree will promote your career, have your company pay for it. If you already have a good people skills, you probably can pull this off. By starting early on your own, you catch two birds with one stone AND get ahead of other people. Here are my recommendation for books:

  • Getting to Yes: Negotiating Agreement Without Giving In
  • Crucial Conversations: Tools for Talking When Stakes Are High
  • The Five Dysfunctions of a Team: A Leadership Fable
  • Getting Things Done: The Art of Stress-Free Productivity
  • The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People: Powerful Lessons in Personal Change
  • How to Win Friends & Influence People
  • Good to Great: Why Some Companies Make the Leap...And Others Don't

Seriously, start now, because I have read these books and I still find myself horrible at the skills these books teach. It probably will take me a lifetime, but I strongly believe that these are skills that have compounding returns, because good social skills attract good people and good people could save you from troubles that you cannot foresee.

Startup:

If you think you are a bit of both mindset (manager, engineer) with a penchant for risks, then start learning javascript. There is a good chance whatever you learn will be useless in the long run, but you will live in excitement that you might be onto something big. Even when you don't have an idea yourself, your (potential) co-founder will be thrilled that you are a javascript expert. If you need some motivation along the way, go read on techcrunch.com on what others are doing.

Or why not try to be all three at the same time? The depth of your knowledge pays, but the breadth of your knowledge pays even more if you know how to knit them together.
