Learning Latex

March 17, 2014 [ dev ] #latex Read in 3 min.

LaTeX is a markup language to put diagrams, mathematical equations, and edit documents in a programmatic way. If you are familiar with markdown, LaTex is markdown on steroids and viagra. Latex is safe for adults.

If you are in the academia (i.e. Ph.D, Post-Doctocral), you really cannot live without latex. Unfortunately, no body actually teach you to use Latex, you pick it up as you go. But unlike some simple markup language, markdown, the syntax is not something you can pick up in a day (okay, if you have, good for you). Still, It is just a such a cool tool that it difficult to ignore even if you don't use it often. So I am going to recommend ways to facilitate the learning process.

My motivation to learn Latex came from taking notes for Algorithms and Database (relational algebra). I learn better by taking notes and I got frustrated because I could not take good notes for those topics. My blog now uses MathJax to render latex now. I have written another article about that.

The best place to start learning is not a wiki page or tutorial (gasp!). You will fall a sleep before you write your first line in Latex. Lo and Behold Mathematics StackExchange page! You've got everything you need: a simple ediotr (cannot be simpler), examples, topics that interest you (hopefully). Try to write some equations that put you to sleep back in high school to start. You can click "Improve this answer" or "Edit" to see the "source code" in Latex.

When you have basics down but can't find example for things that you want to render in Latex, you are encouraged to look for reference online. Now when you look at a wiki page or tutorials, you can skim over details you don't need.

When you are no longer afraid of the syntax and feel its usefulness, you can go ahead and go with more full-featured editor. My editor of choice is TexMaker. Watch at Mrs. Krummel's youtube videos to help you get started.

The next step would be to going to Tex StackExchange page to ask questions and pick up some obscure features that suit your needs.

Basic syntax

$ .. $ for inline equation $$ .. $$ for centered, on-its-own-line equation. Double blank line for a line break - same indentation as the previous sentence. \\: soft break line - no indentation (continued from the previous paragraph)

Basic math notations

^: exponent _: subscript \pi, \alpha, \sin(x), \log{x}, \ln{x}, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt[3]{2} \frac{2}{3}: divide sign between two equations \displacesytle{}: make the equation look bigger by default \{\$\} \left{XXX\right}: put braces around XXX with matching height \left.XXX\right}: put nothing on one side \left{XXX\right.}
