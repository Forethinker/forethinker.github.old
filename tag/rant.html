My thoughts and predictions on bitcoin
March 30, 2014 [ life ] #finance, #rant Read in 5 min.

I never owned any bitcoin. I read about it and understand its technical achievements, but I do not see it replacing our current currency system. For our intend and purposes, lets say bitcoin's market price becomes somewhat stable and it has been all mined up. Even at this point, the government has no way of tracking its transactions and therefore cannot back it up or protect any of its financial transactions. Well, that is the whole point of the cryptocurrrency. It cannot be used to provide FDIC insurance ($250,000 per account per person in case of bank run-ons). So for typical people, it provides no protection at all. So it cannot be embraced by and for the popular mass.

So that leaves the rich and the ultra rich. Surely, they need a system in which they could transfer money discretely for their lobbying and money laundering, right? And with the increasing disparity between rich and poor, it just makes sense to have a magic stash for these people. Barring the possibility that bitcoin replaces the currency, could it find its usefulness here? Maybe. But I see this less than likely to happen, even with a much safer personal vault (e.g. Trucrypt Container variant). Because, it is just not an economically stable vehicle for storing wealth, a.k.a. the perceived value of one's labor.

Right now bitcoin is miles ahead of its competitor in terms of "market cap, popularity, network strength, real economy (products & services), trading volume, wallet software". But believe or not, cryptocurrency will be replicated in the future with ease and there will be much more demand to do so if the bitcoin has reached it mining limit. There is no entity to regulate replication for cryptocurrency, and the government has no incentive to do so when it cannot control it or protect it. If you allowed people to print their set of money, just because \$100 bills have been all printed out, that does not prevent people from printing \$49 bills or \$4 bills or 2 cent coins. You see LiteCoin, DogeCoin, etc keep popping out. This trend will go on and the entire cryptocurrency will collapse in economic sense (but not disappear). It is not viable for people to store their wealth in cryptocurrency, especially so if you are super rich and have got more to lose.

Cryptocrurency can be useful though, if it can be paired with services provided by companies like Facebook, Wikipedia, Google, Quora, Stackoverflow - for rewarding people for doing something that a computer or machine is unable to do. (e.g. reCaptcha) If someone asks a question and the computer is unable to answer it, the person who answers it can be awarded. The cryptocurrency can then be used to ask another question. This will fuel innovation and change how our economy works. But until this system comes along, bitcoin is just a prototype. The company or an entity who creates this system and provide services will come up with his/her own cryptocurrency.

All in all, cyptocurrency will be like collectible cards: Magic, Pocketmon, YugiOh, NBA, NFL, or MLB cards. Some people will want it, like it, trade it, sell it, buy it, or barter it, but its circulation will be limited to one group (like playing cards collectors) and its valuation will be tied to the government backed currency (because most people value protection over privacy). As for me, I don't want to be that kid again who collects cards or coins because it is 'cool'. I still regret trading my lego pieces for Looney Tunes cards back when I was five. I've learn my lesson the "hard" way. And I feel really good about writing this article, because I had a strong impulse to buy an ASIC bitcoin miner before and I knew it wasn't a logical choice.

Using bitcoin to fix DDoS problem? I don't think so

Another thing I want to point out is Chris Dixon's idea on using cryptocurrency to prevent network congestion.. I do not think bitcoin is the way to solve DDoS problem because most problematic DDoS attacks are not initiated by users who are hefty about their internet usage, but rather it is by their hacked computers that triggers the attack simultaneously with other zombie machines at the same time. One network communication is cheap enough that once the attack has happened, the user will not notice that much difference in their billing statements that they were a part of the attack. Even if the cost of network transmission was proportionally expensive to the bandwidth demanded, the distributed cost will make it seem like nothing to benign individuals who are a part of the attack. I believe money (supply/demand) is not the problem here. I am criticizing something here without giving an alternative solution, which goes against my own philosophy about criticizing, but I just had to throw it out there and put some water on bitcoin hype. Chris, by some chance if you are reading this, I like your all of your other articles, man.
