Why spending more time than you save is okay, and when it is not.
May 01, 2013 [ life ] #lifehacker, #automation Read in 3 min.

"XKCD: How Much Time Should You Spend"

I am certainly guilty of trying to automate everything, including keystrokes, but I never calculate the time I invest to get things automated. This is because the benefits I leap is not just the time I save, but the knowledge and expertise I gain in the process. If you want to automate something, you must know how it works, sometimes you also need to know the foundation beneath that makes everything tick.

There are more merits. In this society where sharing is the norm, you may never know who can expand on the knowledge you share. I am certainly glad that there is a makefile for a program compilation. You could actually argue that programming itself is automating. People spent time to convert binary into a assembly, then to a higher language, and so forth - now most consumers' instructions are automated so that it can tell the machines exactly what they want - no flipping wires needed.

I would even argue that teaching someone is automation. Here is a example: helping my parents to fix their computer problems. If I calculated my ROI, I would not have fixed their problems myself and moved on. But I explained things to them along the way. And now, the confidence they gained over the years is priceless. And recently, my mother used iPad to communicate with a real estate agent and my father got everything on PDFs to get things organized and processed. So I think my ROI is positive now, but regardless of all that, I am just so proud them.

There is more to automation than saving time and making profits. And If we calculate ROIs all the time, we will be wrong more often than not and we will fail to be true innovators. Just make sure you are doing your dues to put food on the table and get on rolling with your passion.

The only caution one needs to take when automating tasks is doing a good research of alternatives. For example, there are add-ons for Firefox that merely implements a feature that can be ran by a few keystrokes. I am sure there are people who appreciate the author's altruistic devotion to develop such add-ons, but in the end, the lack of research (both by the creators and users) create noise in innovation and progress - preventing a better solution to be reached and wasting efforts. There is no hard guideline for this, but if your work is not getting any traction, there is a reason for that and do not let your ego or fear get in the way. Take your project down if it means leading people to something better. Well, it is easy said than done, so I am making a reminder to myself here.
